Seed

Your name means “God is with them.”

Panashe, before you read anything else, sit with that. Grace has been with you the whole way. Through every season you were sure you would not survive, something in you kept turning toward the morning.

I made this book because you are one of the most loving, fiercely protective, genuinely good people I know, and you spend that love so freely on everyone around you. You would walk through fire for the people you care about. I have watched you do it.

This book is me asking you to point some of that back at yourself.

You have been strong for a long time. You have survived things that would have flattened most people, and you came out the other side still soft, still kind, still you. That is not luck. That is you.

So this is not a book about fixing you. There is nothing to fix. It is a book about helping you finally see what the rest of us already see.

I have listened to the way you defend the people you love, even the ones who have hurt you, even when there is no one else in the room to hear you do it. The strength and integrity that takes is something most people simply do not have. You have it without even trying. That is who you are when you think no one is watching, and it is one of the most beautiful things about you.

With all my love,
Kev
Before you begin

This is not a book to finish.
It is a book to return to.

There is no right pace. Read one page a week if that is what you have. Skip the pages that feel too heavy today and come back when they do not. Write in the margins. Argue with it. Leave it for a month.

The only thing I ask is that you keep turning the pages, the way a sunflower keeps turning.

Slowly is still turning.

Roots

Where you have been is proof, not a sentence.

You have been through things you did not deserve. People who should have been gentle with you were not. That was real, and it was not your fault, and you do not have to pretend it did not leave a mark.

But here is what I need you to hear once, clearly, so we can move forward:

Your past is evidence of how much you can carry. It is not a prediction of how much you will have to.

The people who made you feel small were wrong about you. Their voice is not the truth about who you are. It was just the loudest voice in the room for a while.

We are going to turn the page now. Not to forget it, but because you are so much more than the worst thing that happened to you.

What the sunflower knows

After the Chernobyl disaster, scientists planted sunflowers in the poisoned ground. The flowers pulled the radiation up out of the earth and into themselves, and bloomed gold anyway. The very thing that grew in the toxic soil became the thing that helped heal it. What you survived did not ruin you. It is becoming your strength.

Turning toward the light

A thought is not a fact.

When you have been told hard things for long enough, your own mind learns to say them to you. It starts to sound like your voice. It is not.

That inner critic feels like the truth because it is familiar, not because it is right.

Here is the first shift, and it changes everything once you feel it: you are not your thoughts. You are the one noticing them. The mind says “you are too much,” or “nothing will ever change,” and you get to stand back and say, “that is a thought I am having. It is not a headline. It is not a verdict.”

You do not have to win the fight with that voice. You just have to stop believing everything it says.

“Change your thoughts and you change your world.”

Norman Vincent Peale

Even on cloudy days

The love you give so freely, learn to give yourself.

You are endlessly gentle with the people you love. When they fail, you comfort them. When they are tired, you carry them. When they are hard on themselves, you are the first to say, “be kinder to yourself.”

Now hear yourself say it.

Self-compassion is not weakness and it is not letting yourself off the hook. It is talking to yourself the way you already talk to everyone you love. It is the thing that actually helps people change, far more than being hard on themselves ever has.

You have been your own harshest critic for long enough. Try being your own friend for a while and see what grows.

What the sunflower knows

A sunflower turns its face to the sun for a reason. The warmth helps it grow, and it draws in the bees it needs to bloom and make seed. It is not vanity. It is survival. Turning toward what nourishes you is not selfish. It is how you were built to bloom.

A letter to you

Write to yourself the way you would write to her.

Imagine a woman you love deeply had lived your exact life. Same hard seasons. Same voices. Same survival. Same tenderness that never went away.

Write her a letter. Tell her what you see in her. Tell her what she got through. Tell her what she deserves now.

Then read it back and put her name at the top: Panashe. Because that letter was always for you.

Facing the sun

You are allowed to imagine a good future. And you are allowed to have one.

I know part of you is scared that you will turn forty and nothing will have changed, and you will still feel exactly like this. I know that fear is heavy and it is real.

So let me say this gently. That fear is not a prophecy. It is what happens when someone has been through so much that they have lost the ability to picture things being good, not because good things cannot come, but because the imagining muscle got tired.

We are going to rebuild that muscle. Not with fantasy. Not “manifest your dream life.” Just with the quiet, believable picture of an ordinary good day that is genuinely yours.

You do not have to believe in it yet. You just have to let yourself picture it.

“Keep your face to the sunshine and you cannot see a shadow.”

Helen Keller
A letter from you

A letter from you, on a good ordinary day, years from now.

Picture yourself a few years ahead, on a day when things have shifted. Nothing dramatic. Just a normal day where you feel settled. Where the heavy thing is quieter.

Write a short letter from that version of you, back to the you reading this today. Let her tell you what her ordinary morning looks like now, what small things got her here, and the one thing she wants you to hold onto on the hard days.

Keep it small and real. A good coffee. A calm chest. A morning she was not dreading. The future does not have to be extraordinary to be worth reaching for. It just has to be yours, and good.

Daily light

Small practices, done often, on the ordinary days.

You do not need to overhaul your life. You need a few small things you turn toward, the way a sunflower turns, a little each day.

In the morning

Before the day grabs you, three slow breaths. On the exhale, quietly: “I am here. I made it to today. That is enough to start.”

Sometime in the day

Name one thing, however small, that was good. Gratitude is not pretending the hard is not there. It is refusing to let it be the only thing you see.

At night

Name one kind thing you did, or one thing you got through. You are allowed to be proud of surviving an ordinary day.

What I see

What I see when I look at you.

Read this on the days the voice is loud.

You are kind in a way that costs you something, and you do it anyway.

You believe the best in people. You see the good in them that everyone else tends to overlook, and so often you are the only one who does.

You are fiercely, beautifully protective of the people you love.

Your compassion and your empathy are effortless. Not performed, not forced. It is simply who you are, and people feel it the moment they are near you. When you speak, people listen. They believe that you care about them, because you actually do.

You are strong. Not the loud kind. The kind that survived quietly and kept your softness through it. That is the rarest strength there is.

You are beautiful, and not only to look at. You are beautiful in the way you show up for people.

Here is something I need you to hold onto. Your purpose does not have to be anything outside of the natural gifts God already put in you. The way you connect with people and genuinely help them is not a small thing you do on the side. That is the work. That is the gift. It has been there the whole time.

You have so much good in you that the world has not even seen yet. It is coming. You are just early.

You survived the worst of it. The rest of your life gets to be different.

When it is hard

When it is hard, read this.

Some days will be heavy. That does not mean you are failing or going backward. Sunflowers droop before they bloom again. It is part of it.

On those days, you do not have to fix anything. Just read the page before this one, slowly. Take three breaths. And reach out to one person. You do not have to carry it alone, and reaching out is strength, not weakness.

You are loved by more people than the hard days let you feel. I am one of them, and I am not going anywhere.

And if a day ever gets darker than a book can hold, please tell someone you trust, or someone whose job it is to help. There is no shame in it. The bravest thing you can do is let yourself be helped the way you have always helped everyone else.

The field is yours

Here is the truest thing about a sunflower. On grey days, when the sky is thick with cloud and there is no sun to be seen at all, the young sunflower keeps turning to follow the sun anyway. It cannot see the light. But something inside it, a quiet clock all its own, keeps it facing the place where the light will be.

It does not need to see the sun to keep believing in it.

That is what I am asking of you, Panashe. On the days you cannot feel any warmth at all, keep facing the direction of the good that is coming. And on the days even that feels impossible, turn toward the people who love you. We will be your sun until you can feel your own again.

You were never broken. You were a seed the whole time. And seeds do not stay in the dark. They rise.

Keep turning toward the light.

With all my love,
Kev
